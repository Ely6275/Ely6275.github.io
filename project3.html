DWG. EH-03 — EMBEDDED SYSTEMS / SENSING

Scissor Lift Collision Warning System

A dual-LiDAR sensor network that alerts scissor lift operators before collisions — 8 self-contained ESP32 sensor units, sub-0.1 second alert latency, and 0.7% measured distance error. Built for Skanska USA Building, Inc. through Duke's Engineering Design POD program; the prototype passed all five client design tests.

01 — SYSTEM OVERVIEW

At a Glance

Scissor lifts are large, accelerate suddenly, and leave operators with poor visibility at corners and overhead. Our system surrounds the lift with two complementary LiDAR types that beep faster as clearance shrinks — starting at 12 inches — so the operator hears proximity without looking at a display.

SENSING — CHIP LIDAR ×6

Mounted magnetically on the basket corners and base. Each unit has a 3D field of view, driven by an ESP32-C3 SuperMini on a 9V battery.

SENSING — 360° LIDAR ×2

Front and rear sweeping units scanning a full 360° arc, with a servo tilt that extends vertical coverage. Standard ESP32 in a weatherproof control box.

EMBEDDED ALERT LOGIC

Firmware distance thresholds trigger onboard buzzers; beep frequency scales with proximity. Measured response: under 0.1 seconds from detection to alert.

DEPLOYABILITY

Every unit is self-contained in a custom 3D-printed enclosure with magnet mount — operators place or remove sensors in seconds, no tools, no client-side code.

03 — FIRMWARE

Firmware Deep Dive — 360° LiDAR Unit

I wrote the firmware for the 360° sweeping LiDAR unit in C++ on the ESP32, built around FreeRTOS real-time multitasking — LiDAR ingest, servo sweep, and alerting run as independent tasks that communicate through event-driven notifications instead of polling.

CONCURRENCY — DUAL-CORE RTOS

Three FreeRTOS tasks pinned across both ESP32 cores with explicit priorities: LiDAR ingest (priority 4) and servo sweep (priority 3) share core 0, while the buzzer task runs on core 1 — alert timing never blocks scan processing, and vice versa.

ISR-SAFE TASK SIGNALING

The buzzer task sleeps on ulTaskNotifyTake and is woken from the LiDAR data path via vTaskNotifyGiveFromISR with a yield request — zero CPU wasted on polling. The servo receives STOP/RESUME commands as notification bit flags.

SENSOR FUSION — POSE-AWARE FILTERING

Every LiDAR return is validated against the servo's current pointing angle: dynamic ignore zones (20–160° or 200–340°, depending on sweep direction) reject readings from behind the scan plane, eliminating false positives.

UART DRIVER & ACTUATOR CONTROL

The LD14P LiDAR streams over a dedicated hardware UART with the RX buffer raised to 1 KB for burst traffic, integrated through a callback-driven C++ driver class. The servo runs on calibrated 500–2400 µs PWM pulses.

Control Flow Highlights

  • Streaming minimum-distance reduction — each full revolution is reduced to its closest valid return on the fly (zero-readings rejected, points downsampled 20:1), so obstacle detection runs in constant memory at scan rate.
  • Servo hold-and-resume state machine — the sweep loop checks for a STOP notification without blocking at every 5° step; on detection it freezes the scan head at its current angle and suspends until the obstacle clears, then resumes mid-sweep exactly where it left off.
  • Graduated proximity alerts — beep intervals tighten from 500 ms to 50 ms across the 500 mm → 150 mm approach envelope, encoding distance into sound so the operator hears how close they are, not just that something is there.
  • Object-oriented driver architecture — the LiDAR is abstracted behind a driver class with registered callbacks for scan points, packets, serial I/O, and errors, keeping hardware specifics isolated from the detection logic.

04 — PROCESS

Engineering Design Process

We followed a structured decomposition and evaluation framework — from problem definition through sensor trade studies, ranked design criteria, and a formal test plan.

Our formal problem statement to Skanska USA (September 2025) identified scissor lift collisions as a major safety and property-damage risk: lifts are large, accelerate suddenly, and operators have limited visibility — especially above the platform and at corners.

The system had to generalize to any construction company: no client-side code editing, secure magnetic attachment, and battery life beyond 16 hours of continuous operation.

We evaluated five sensor types with Pugh screening and scoring matrices, weighted across precision, environmental interference, field of vision, battery consumption, and cost:

LiDAR scored highest — its precision and field of vision were critical for reliable detection despite the cost premium. mmWave radar remained the secondary candidate through prototyping, since the matrix doesn't capture non-linear cost thresholds.

Design criteria ranked by pairwise comparison, most to least important:

  • Alert audibility — 90–95 dB, at least 10 dB above ambient construction noise
  • Timely alerting — alert within 0.1 s of an object entering the field of vision
  • Varying clearance alerts — distinct beep frequencies at 1 in, 3 in, and 6 in thresholds
  • Ease of setup — no code editing by the client, minimal on-site configuration
  • Battery life — at least 16 hours of operation, recharge in 4–5 hours
  • Sensor precision — detection distance error ≤ 10%
  • Clearance / braking — warning at specified clearance; future auto-stop integration
  • Minimal false positives — zero random triggers over a 30-minute test window

Four tests validated the prototype against the design criteria:

  • Sensor detection — sweep a 5×5 in sample across the horizontal plane; measure detection distance and angular FOV. Target: <5% distance error, ≥55° FOV each direction.
  • Alert system — drop an object in front of the sensor on video; count delay frames and measure buzzer volume. Target: <0.1 s delay, 90–95 dB.
  • Setup process — time operators placing all units on marked spots. Target: <5 min new users, <2 min experienced.
  • Battery consumption — 10-hour continuous runs with LED indicator, repeated 3×. Target: ≥10 h per charge, recharge in 6 h.
